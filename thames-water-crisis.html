üèõÔ∏è Part 1: How We Got Here‚ÄîThe 1989 Setup

The Blank Slate Handoff

On December 1, 1989, the UK government did something remarkable: they gave away ten regional water monopolies. Not sold. Gave away.

The government wrote off ¬£5 billion in existing debt and provided another ¬£1.5 billion as a "green dowry" to help the new companies meet environmental standards. The water companies started their private lives with completely unleveraged balance sheets‚Äîa blank slate that subsequent owners would exploit to the maximum.

The stated goals were textbook free-market ideology:

  • Eliminate the need for public borrowing
  • Introduce private-sector efficiency and innovation
  • Create a competitive market for capital investment

But here's what I realised as I traced through the consequences: They mispriced the risk.

The Fatal Flaw in the Design

A utility with 50-100 year assets (pipes and treatment plants) and stable, inflation-linked regulated cash flows has enormous natural capacity for debt. By handing new owners a completely deleveraged balance sheet, the government created a massive financial arbitrage opportunity.

Rational financial actors would inevitably ask: "Why use expensive equity when we can borrow against guaranteed cash flows?"

And that's exactly what happened. But first, they needed a regulatory framework that would protect lenders.

‚öôÔ∏è Part 2: The Regulatory Machine‚ÄîHow Ofwat Created Perfect Collateral

The RAB: A Government-Backed Promise

The cornerstone of UK water regulation is something called the Regulatory Asset Base (RAB). Think of it as a running total of everything the company has invested in infrastructure, adjusted for inflation.

Here's how it works:

  1. Step 1: You spend ¬£100 million building a new treatment plant
  2. Step 2: Ofwat (the regulator) adds that ¬£100 million to your RAB
  3. Step 3: Every year, Ofwat indexes your RAB to inflation (typically RPI or CPIH)
  4. Step 4: Ofwat lets you earn a regulated return (historically 3.5-4.5% real) on that inflated RAB
  5. Step 5: You pass those costs to customers through bills

From an investor's perspective, this is basically a government bond with extra steps. You get:

  • Guaranteed, inflation-linked returns
  • Captive customer base (people can't not pay for water)
  • Regulatory protection from political interference
  • Extremely long-term cash flow visibility

But here's where it gets interesting: The RAB also makes perfect collateral for debt.

How the RAB Became a Borrowing Machine

Let's say your RAB is ¬£10 billion. Even if your company only has ¬£2 billion in equity, lenders see that ¬£10 billion asset base as security. The cash flows are protected. The asset value rises with inflation. Political expropriation is nearly impossible (would require compensating bondholders at full value).

So you can borrow way more. And that's exactly what happened. But to really maximize the leverage, you needed one more financial innovation: ring-fencing.

üîó Part 3: The Financial Weapon‚ÄîHow Ring-Fencing Became a Loophole

What Is Whole Business Securitization?

In 2001, Welsh Water pioneered something called Whole Business Securitization (WBS). Thames Water adopted it in 2006-2007 after Macquarie bought the company.

The structure looks like this:

The regulated utility (TWUL) is legally isolated from parent companies through a "special purpose vehicle" structure. Everything the company owns‚Äîpipes, treatment works, reservoirs, licenses, customer relationships‚Äîis pledged as security for bonds.

Cash flows through a strict "waterfall":

  1. First Priority: Operating costs (keep the water flowing)
  2. Second Priority: Maintenance capital expenditure
  3. Third Priority: Debt service (interest and principal on bonds)
  4. Fourth Priority: Regulatory and tax obligations
  5. Final Priority: Distributions to parent companies (dividends)
Here's the key insight I had: This structure was sold as protecting bondholders, but it actually enabled extraction.

The Accounting Trick That Made It Work

Because the bonds are issued by the regulated operating company (not the parent), they benefit from:

  • Security package: Comprehensive fixed and floating charge over all assets
  • Covenant protection: Multiple financial trip-wires that restrict management if breached
  • Ratings boost: The ring-fence meant bonds could achieve A/A- ratings despite the parent company loading up with additional debt

From 2006-2017, Thames Water's WBS bonds maintained investment-grade ratings even as total group leverage climbed toward 80%. Why? Because rating agencies looked at the operating company in isolation and saw:

  • Stable cash flows ‚úì
  • Ring-fence protection ‚úì
  • Regulatory oversight ‚úì

What they missed was happening one level up: parent companies were loading the entire structure with junior debt, secured by dividends paid up from TWUL. The upstream entities held their own debt‚Äîtypically unsecured or junior to the OpCo bonds. These entities had minimal operations; their role was financial structuring. They serviced their debt through dividends paid from TWUL.

This created a problem: if cash flow is insufficient, equity holders are first to suffer, but the company must service bonds first, potentially cutting corners on maintenance and environmental compliance.

How to Extract ¬£2.8 Billion in 11 Years

When Macquarie's consortium bought Thames Water in 2006 for ¬£8 billion, I initially thought: "That's a lot to pay for a utility." Then I looked at what they did next.

The Dividend Extraction:

The numbers here get complicated because of inter-company loans and financial arrangements. But here's what I found:

  • Macquarie's stated figure: ¬£1.1 billion total (¬£879m dividends + ¬£277m interest on shareholder loans)
  • Independent analyses: Up to ¬£2.8 billion when including all inter-company transfers and loan arrangements

For context, during this same period:

  • Capital expenditure: ~¬£11 billion
  • But approximately ¬£57 of every ¬£100 spent on CapEx was matched by ¬£57 spent on finance costs (interest and dividends)

The discrepancy highlights the complexity of the group structure. Multiple layers of entities, inter-company loans, and financial arrangements make it difficult to definitively quantify total "value extraction."

The question I kept asking: Was this investment, or just financial engineering?

How the Numbers Worked

Here's the mechanism that made this possible:

  1. Step 1: Make capital investments (build new infrastructure)
  2. Step 2: Those investments get added to the RAB
  3. Step 3: The inflated RAB generates higher allowed revenues
  4. Step 4: Borrow against the higher RAB to fund dividends
  5. Step 5: Repeat

During periods of high inflation (like 2022-2024), this became especially profitable. The RCV grew from indexation faster than debt service costs, creating "indexation uplift" that appeared as non-cash income in financial statements. This uplift could be distributed as dividends even though no actual cash had been generated.

What Was Actually Happening to the Infrastructure

While the financial engineering accelerated, something else was happening on the ground. Leakage improvements during 2007-2012 were real. Thames reduced leakage from 860 Ml/d to 680 Ml/d. Service quality metrics held steady.

But after 2017‚Äîright after Macquarie's exit‚Äîperformance deteriorated sharply:

  • Pollution incidents surged: 470 incidents in 2023-24, dramatically higher than peers
  • Asset health declined: Years of deferred maintenance created a backlog requiring urgent, costly reactive repairs
  • Service quality dropped: The company slipped toward crisis, culminating in their 1-star EPA rating (the lowest possible)

This timing suggests that maintenance and operational resilience were deferred during the period when cash flow was tight but the financial engineering had served its purpose. The problems emerged only after the financial engineers had extracted value and exited.

üôà Part 5: The Regulatory Failure‚ÄîWhy Ofwat Let This Happen

The Notional Company Fiction

Ofwat sets prices based on a "notional company" with assumed financial characteristics:

  • Notional Gearing: Historically 55-60% (debt as % of RCV)
  • Notional Credit Rating: Investment-grade (typically BBB+/A-)
  • Notional Capital Structure: Balanced mix of debt and equity

But here's the problem: This is purely theoretical. Companies can choose whatever capital structure they want. If Thames operates at 80% gearing while Ofwat prices for 55%, Thames can keep the difference. More efficient, right? That's how it was sold.

Except when things go wrong, the notional 55% company would be fine while the actual 80% company faces insolvency.

The 15-Year Blind Spot

Throughout the 1990s and 2000s, Ofwat largely viewed capital structure and dividend policy as "market outcomes" outside its direct regulatory purview. The thinking was:

  • If companies made imprudent decisions, market discipline (credit downgrades, inability to refinance) would constrain them
  • License conditions required "financeability" but didn't specify how

This proved completely wrong. Three failures of market discipline became apparent:

  1. Information Asymmetry: Companies had vastly better information about their true financial position than regulators or markets. Complex offshore corporate structures deliberately designed to obscure financial health made transparency nearly impossible. (Debt structuring)
  2. Long Time Horizons: Water infrastructure problems emerge slowly, allowing short-term financial engineering to extract value before operational consequences manifest. By the time financial stress became undeniable, equity value had already been extracted.
  3. Implicit Guarantee: Everyone assumed essential services couldn't be allowed to fail, creating moral hazard. Private owners could optimize for short-term returns and extraction, confident that if things went wrong, the state would step in to protect customers and bondholders.

When Did Ofwat Wake Up?

2019: The Turning Point (PR19 Price Review)

By the mid-2010s, concern was growing about financial engineering in the sector. Reports emerged detailing high dividend payments from highly-leveraged companies (Politically sensitive after the 2008-09 financial crisis), complex offshore corporate structures, and growing gaps between notional and actual gearing.

Thames Water became the poster child. Following Macquarie's exit in 2017, the company hit a record ¬£20.3 million fine for sewage leaks that had occurred under Macquarie's ownership‚Äîsuggesting problems had been concealed or deferred.

For the first time in PR19, Ofwat conducted detailed assessments of companies' financial structures, warning those with high gearing. They introduced enhanced penalty mechanisms (ODIs - Outcome Delivery Incentives) creating significant financial downside risk for poor performance.

December 2019: License Condition P30

Ofwat established principles for dividend payments:

  • Principle 1: Dividends should reflect company performance, demonstrating link to service quality and operational effectiveness
  • Principle 2: Decisions should be transparent and understood by stakeholders
  • Principle 3: Companies should explain consistency with long-term business planning
  • Principle 4: Dividends should maintain both current and long-term financial resilience

Crucially, P30 was principles-based, not a hard cap. Companies had to publish statements justifying dividends, but Ofwat's enforcement was reactive.

Thames violated P30 with two dividend payments in late 2023/early 2024 totaling ¬£195.8 million. Ofwat launched an enforcement case, ultimately:

  • Imposing an ¬£18.2 million penalty
  • Ordering ¬£131.3 million clawed back through price control adjustments

But this came after billions had already been extracted. The tougher approach now squeezed companies that were already fragile from years of aggressive financial management.

üìâ Part 6: The Breaking Point‚ÄîWhen Everything Stopped Working

PR24: The Regulatory Squeeze

The current price review (PR24) for the 2025-2030 period became the catalyst for Thames's financial collapse. Here's what happened:

The Investment Gap:

Ofwat identified ¬£144 billion of sector-wide investment needs, with Thames requiring approximately ¬£19 billion to meet environmental obligations:

  • Reducing sewage spills by 29%
  • Preventing nutrient pollution
  • Fixing decades of deferred leakage repairs

The Efficiency Challenge:

In July 2024, Ofwat's draft determination allowed average household bill increases of 21% over five years, front-loaded with particularly large increases in 2025-26. For Thames customers, this meant bills rising by ¬£152 over five years, or approximately ¬£17/month initially.

But then Ofwat did something that shocked Thames: they challenged the company's business plan, imposing a ¬£4 billion reduction in expected capital expenditure allowances. Ofwat argued the company could deliver required investment more efficiently. Thames countered that the determination was financially unachievable given high inflation, unprecedented infrastructure demands, and the need to service existing debt.

The Regulatory Squeeze Explained

Here's why this mattered so much:

The draft determination became the explicit trigger for Thames's crisis. The parent company stated that "uncertainty concerning the outcome of the PR24 price review and financing of the business" necessitated the full ¬£4.3 billion impairment of its investment in TWUL. In other words: the allowed revenues were insufficient to support both the required investment and the debt service obligations.

When macro shocks hit (inflation, rising interest rates), the 80% geared company struggled while the notional 55% geared company‚Äîwhich Ofwat's prices were based on‚Äîwould have been fine.

March 2025: The Covenant Breach

Then came the technical trigger that made the crisis official. Thames Water's WBS structure includes multiple financial covenants that trigger restrictions if breached. The critical one is the PMICR (Post-Maintenance Interest Cover Ratio).

Here's how PMICR works:

PMICR = (Operating Cash Flow - RCV Depreciation) / Secured Interest Expense

Think of it as a stress test: After you've paid to run the company and set aside money for depreciation (the pipes wearing out), do you have enough cash left to pay bondholders?

Thames's covenant required: 1.1x minimum

In March 2025, they reported: 1.09x

That's a razor-thin 9% margin. Cash generation covered interest by only 9%‚Äîa margin providing no cushion for unexpected costs or revenue shortfalls.

What the Breach Actually Meant

When PMICR fell to 1.09x, the securitization covenants automatically prevented distributions from TWUL to parent companies. This protected bondholders but effectively rendered equity worthless.

The dual lock-up (covenant breach + license conditions triggered by credit downgrades in April 2024) meant Thames was completely unable to move money to the holding companies, which then defaulted on their own unsecured debt obligations.

The WBS structure functioned exactly as designed‚Äîprotecting senior creditors while concentrating losses on equity and junior creditors.

And this brought us to October 2025, when Thames ran out of runway entirely and had to ask creditors for emergency funding.

üí• Part 7: The ¬£4.3 Billion Smoking Gun‚ÄîWhat the Impairment Reveals

Understanding the Write-Down

In March 2024, Thames Water's parent company (Thames Water Utilities Holdings Limited) recognized a ¬£4.4 billion pre-tax loss, comprising:

  • ¬£4.3 billion impairment of the investment in the operating company (TWUL)
  • Effectively zero equity value at parent level after impairment

This wasn't a trading loss. This was a one-time recognition of structural failure.

How Impairment Testing Works

Under IAS 36 (International Accounting Standard for asset impairment), entities must assess whether there's any indication an asset may be impaired at each reporting date. If indicators exist, a formal impairment test must be conducted, comparing:

  • Carrying Amount: The book value of the asset on the balance sheet (historical investment plus retained earnings)
  • vs.
  • Recoverable Amount: The higher of:
    • Fair value less costs of disposal (market value)
    • Value in use (present value of future cash flows)

If recoverable amount < carrying amount, an impairment loss must be recognized.

Thames's Impairment Analysis

The parent company held an investment in TWUL with a carrying value of ¬£4,250 million as of March 31, 2023. One year later, this was written down entirely to ¬£nil.

The Stated Trigger: Directors explicitly cited "uncertainty concerning the outcome of the PR24 price review and financing of the business" as the impairment trigger. This is critical‚Äîit confirms that regulatory policy risk, not operational trading losses, caused the complete destruction of asset value.

The Valuation Methodology

The company likely used "Value in Use" methodology, calculating the present value of future cash flows that the investment would generate. This calculation would have included:

  • Expected cash flows from TWUL operations
  • Minus: Required capital expenditure (~¬£20-24 billion over AMP8-AMP9, the next two 5-year regulatory periods)
  • Minus: Debt service obligations (¬£16+ billion debt at ~4-5% average cost)
  • Discounted at an appropriate rate

The conclusion: Future cash flows from TWUL, after servicing debt and funding required investment under PR24 allowances, would provide no residual value to equity holders.

What This Means in Plain English

Management determined that future cash flows from TWUL would provide no residual value to equity holders after meeting all obligations. The equity retained genuine value until PR24's efficiency challenges and regulatory squeeze suddenly rendered the structure unviable. This raises the question: when did value actually disappear?

Two theories:

  • Gradual Erosion Theory: Value eroded gradually over 2006-2024 as operational issues accumulated and financial structure became more fragile, but was only recognized in 2024 when PR24 made it impossible to ignore.
  • Sudden Collapse Theory: The equity retained genuine value until PR24's efficiency challenges and regulatory squeeze suddenly rendered the structure unviable, causing rapid collapse.
The evidence weight suggests gradual erosion masked by financial engineering and favorable regulatory periods, with PR24 as the catalyst exposing what was already a fragile position. The ¬£4.3bn impairment likely represents accumulated value destruction over many years, finally recognized.

The Tax Implications

Crucially, the accounts reflect this as a "non-deductible expense (impairment on investment)." This restriction on tax deductibility demonstrates that HMRC views this as a failure of the investment structure itself, not an allowable trading loss. The holding company bears the full cash cost of the write-down without immediate fiscal relief, further confirming this is capital structure failure, not operational difficulty.

üåç Part 8: The Comparison That Changes Everything

Here's what finally made everything click for me: I looked at how other countries do this. The UK's model of full private ownership and operation of water utilities is virtually unique among developed economies. Most OECD countries maintain some form of public ownership or control, even when private sector involvement exists in operations.

Let me show you three alternatives that actually work:

The French Model: Private Operations, Public Assets

Structure:

France pioneered a public-private partnership model where:

  • Asset Ownership: Municipalities retain ownership of all physical infrastructure (pipes, treatment plants, reservoirs)
  • Operations: Private companies (primarily Veolia and Suez) operate systems through long-term contracts

Contract Types:

  • Affermage (Lease) Contract:
    • Duration: 10-15 years
    • Municipality finances major capital investments
    • Private operator responsible for operations, maintenance, customer service
    • Risk mostly borne by municipality
  • Concession Contract:
    • Duration: 20-30 years
    • Municipality retains asset ownership
    • Private operator finances AND operates, taking more risk
    • Assets revert to public ownership at contract end

Financial Characteristics:

  • Veolia (2023): Net Debt/EBITDA of 2.74x‚Äîsignificantly lower leverage than Thames (effective leverage >6x)
  • Suez (2023): Net Debt/EBITDA of 3.7x‚Äîmoderate leverage

The Key Difference: Private operators earn returns on operations and efficiency, not asset ownership. This limits incentive to financially engineer the asset base while accessing private operational expertise.

Performance:

  • Non-Revenue Water (Water they cant sell): 21.3% (still high, similar to England)
  • Water Quality: Excellent (>99% EU compliance)
  • Tariffs: ‚Ç¨3.48/m¬≥ (moderate, lower than UK)

Limitations:

The French model has faced criticism:

  • Contractual Complexity: Requires sophisticated municipal oversight capabilities
  • Corruption Risks: Historical cases of bid-rigging and political influence in contract awards
  • Re-municipalization Trend: Since 2000, over 100 French municipalities brought services back in-house, including Paris in 2010, citing high costs and lack of transparency

The German Model: Municipal Ownership Dominance

Structure:

Water provision in Germany is overwhelmingly a public function:

  • Over 5,500 municipal utility systems (Stadtwerke)
  • Highly decentralized: Local control reflecting regional preferences
  • Multi-utility model: Often combined with electricity, gas, district heating under one municipal entity

Financing Approach:

  • Retained Earnings: Main source, often cross-subsidized from profitable energy divisions
  • Municipal Budgets: Direct tax revenue can supplement
  • Public Finance Banks: Low-cost loans from institutions like KfW (development bank)
  • Profit Motive: Secondary to service quality and cost recovery

Performance:

  • Non-Revenue Water (Water they cant sell): 7.2%‚Äîworld-class efficiency
  • Water Quality: Excellent (>99.7% EU compliance)
  • Tariffs: ‚Ç¨4.66/m¬≥‚Äîhighest in comparative studies
  • Infrastructure: Over 60% predates 1990, requiring significant renewal investment

Key Strengths:

  • Extremely high service quality and technical excellence
  • Direct democratic accountability to local communities
  • Long-term perspective aligned with infrastructure lifecycles
  • No pressure for dividend extraction

Weaknesses:

  • High costs passed to consumers
  • Limited economies of scale due to fragmentation
  • Requires strong municipal governance capacity
  • Less innovation compared to competitive markets

The Dutch Model: Public Water Boards

Structure:

The Netherlands employs a unique governance model rooted in centuries-old democratic water management traditions:

  • Regional Public Water Boards (Waterschappen):
    • Some of the oldest democratic institutions in the country (dating to 13th century)
    • Governance: Elected representatives of stakeholders (residents, farmers, businesses)
    • Legal Status: Public bodies with authority to levy dedicated water taxes
  • Private Sector Role: Prohibited by law from owning water supply systems

Financing:

  • Dedicated Water Taxes: Direct levies providing transparent funding link
  • NWB Bank: Public sector bank owned by water boards themselves, providing low-cost capital
  • Full Cost Recovery: Rates set to cover operations and capital needs without profit motive

Performance:

  • Non-Revenue Water (Water they cant sell): 6.0%‚Äîamong world's lowest
  • Water Quality: World-leading standards
  • Tariffs: ‚Ç¨3.87/m¬≥‚Äîmoderate/high
  • Efficiency: "Polder Model" of stakeholder collaboration drives continuous improvement

Key Strengths:

  • Technical Excellence: World-leading leakage control and operational efficiency
  • Long-term Sustainability: Democratic governance ensures intergenerational perspective
  • Transparency: Direct link between taxes, funding, and service
  • Innovation: Despite public ownership, strong culture of technical innovation

Contextual Factors:

The Dutch model works within specific context:

  • Existential Water Challenges: Much of Netherlands below sea level; water management is national survival issue
  • Cultural Consensus: Broad societal agreement on public provision
  • High Trust: Strong civic institutions and low corruption
  • Small Scale: Compact geography makes coordination easier

What the International Evidence Shows

Looking at this data, three insights jumped out at me:

  1. Public Ownership ‚â† Inefficiency

    Germany and Netherlands demonstrate that public ownership doesn't inherently mean inefficiency. Their leakage rates (6-7%) vastly outperform privatized England (23%), contradicting claims that only private ownership drives operational excellence.

  2. Ownership vs. Financing Are Separate Questions

    The French model shows private operational expertise can be harnessed through contracts while retaining public asset ownership. This prevents using infrastructure as a financial engineering vehicle while accessing private efficiency.

  3. Financial Structure Matters More Than Ownership

    Welsh Water proves the point: using WBS (efficient financing tool) within not-for-profit ownership (no dividend pressure) produces sustainable outcomes. The problem isn't the financial tool per se, but misaligned incentives when combined with short-term equity ownership.

Could Alternative Models Have Prevented Thames's Crisis?

  • Welsh Water Model (Already UK-Based): Definitely. Welsh Water demonstrates that eliminating shareholder dividends while using efficient financing (WBS) is viable within existing UK regulatory framework. Thames could have been structured this way in 2006.
  • French-Style Public-Private Partnership: Likely yes. Keeping assets in public ownership while contracting operation to private companies would have prevented using infrastructure as debt collateral for shareholder extraction.

Would investment have been lower? Privatization advocates claim only private capital could fund ¬£150+ billion in required improvements. However:

  • France and Germany achieved similar investment levels through public/mixed models
  • Low government borrowing costs (1-2%) make public financing cheaper than equity returns
  • The apparent "efficiency" of private capital often reflected financial engineering, not real cost advantages

The Counterfactual

What if Thames had maintained 55% gearing? If Thames had maintained the 55% gearing Ofwat assumed in its pricing models, they would have:

  • Paid approximately ¬£5 billion less in interest over 2006-2024
  • Had more equity cushion to absorb PR24 efficiency challenges
  • Likely avoided covenant breach
  • Maintained financial resilience

The additional ¬£5 billion in debt (from 55% to 80%+ gearing) primarily funded shareholder distributions, not infrastructure improvements. This suggests the WBS model's efficiency (cheap debt) created fragility that eventually outweighed the benefits.

What I've Learned

Spending weeks analyzing this crisis, a few insights stick with me:

The Invisibility of Gradual Erosion:

Value was destroyed slowly from 2006-2024, masked by financial engineering and favorable regulatory periods. The ¬£4.3bn impairment in 2024 didn't reflect sudden collapse‚Äîit acknowledged what had been true for years but was invisible on balance sheets.

The Power of Incentives:

Welsh Water and Thames had similar tools (WBS, efficient debt financing) but radically different incentives (not-for-profit vs. private equity). That difference explains divergent outcomes more than any other factor.

Put another way: WBS + no dividends = sustainable. WBS + aggressive extraction = crisis. The financing tool isn't the problem‚Äîmisaligned incentives are.

The Cost of Complexity:

Thames's multi-layered offshore structure wasn't an accident‚Äîit obscured financial reality from regulators, investors, and the public. By the time opacity gave way to transparency, value had been extracted and equity was worthless.

The Long Shadow of 1989:

The decision to privatize debt-free created enormous borrowing capacity that subsequent owners inevitably exploited. Perhaps the crisis was pre-programmed by that initial setup‚Äîhanding someone a blank slate and then being surprised when they maximized leverage.

The International Counterfactual:

German and Dutch leakage rates (6-7%) vs. England's (23%) is the stat that haunts me. It demolishes the claim that "only privatization drives efficiency." Public ownership doesn't inherently mean inefficiency‚Äîit's about governance, incentives, and long-term thinking.

üìñ Appendix: Key Terms

  • RAB/RCV (Regulatory Asset Base / Regulated Capital Value): The cumulative value of past infrastructure investment, indexed to inflation, used by Ofwat to determine allowed returns.
  • WBS (Whole Business Securitisation): Financial structure ring-fencing the regulated utility and issuing secured bonds with priority claim on all revenues.
  • Gearing: Net debt as percentage of RCV. Measure of financial leverage.
  • PMICR (Post-Maintenance Interest Cover Ratio): (Operating cash flow - RCV depreciation) / Interest expense. Measure of solvency.
  • PR24: Price Review for 2024, determining allowed revenues for 2025-2030 regulatory period (AMP8).
  • ODIs (Outcome Delivery Incentives): Performance-based rewards and penalties linked to service quality metrics.
  • SAR (Special Administration Regime): Insolvency procedure for essential utilities, prioritizing service continuity.
  • IAS 36: International accounting standard for asset impairment testing.
